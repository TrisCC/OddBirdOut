## Odd Bird Out: An Exploration of Cyber Ostracism

By Tristan Cotino, Aidan Ferentinos and Huub Speijkers

# From Digital Communication to Cyber Ostracism

At the start of the course, our group explored the idea of gaps in digital communication. We were interested in situations where a lack of a response still seems to communicate something, like waiting for a reply or seeing that a message has been read but not answered. These situations interested us because it shows that a lack of a response doesn’t necessarily feel like nothing is happening. Especially in digital communication, silence can become something people interpret and fill with their own meaning.  
After presenting our ideas in class and discussing this direction with our project supervisor, Max van Duijn, we narrowed our focus to the concept of cyber ostracism. Cyber ostracism refers to the experience of being ignored, excluded or rejected in a digital environment. This gave us a clearer scientific basis to ground our project in.

# The problem: Exclusion in Digital Environments

The scientific insight we chose is that social exclusion does not need to be explicitly stated in order to be felt. Humans are highly sensitive to signs of being left out, because belonging to a group is a basic social need. In face-to-face social interactions, communication is supported by non-verbal cues like tone of voice, facial expression, eye contact and body language. These cues can provide us with extra information about what is happening and what the other person's intentions might be.

Digital environments often lack these cues. This leaves social interactions more up for interpretation. Someone may not know whether they are intentionally being ignored, whether the other person is busy, whether the system is limiting their interaction or whether they are just reading too much into the situation. This creates a gap between what is actually happening and what the user feels is happening. The digital interface becomes an intermediary between communicating people, but it can’t fully encompass the social situation and therefore leaves a lot of uncertainty up for interpretation by the user.

That uncertainty and interpretation is very important to our project. In digital environments exclusion can happen very subtly. For example, it can happen when a message isn’t answered or when a profile receives fewer likes than another. These signals may look small from the outside, but on a personal level they can create a strong feeling of being excluded. Thus, the emotional response is not only because of the exclusion itself, but also the uncertainty around it. The user is left to fill in the gap in meaning.

# Why is this insight interesting?

Cyberball, developed by Williams and Jarvis (2006), shows how little is needed to create the feeling of being ostracised. In Cyberball, participants believe they are playing a simple online ball tossing game with two other players. After receiving the ball a few times, the other players begin passing only to each other. Even though the game is very simple and the other players are fictional, excluded participants reported lower belonging, self-esteem, control and meaningful existence.

Wolf et al. (2014) developed this idea further with Ostracism Online, a paradigm based on social media. Instead of being excluded from a ball-tossing game, participants created a personal profile and received varying amounts of attention from other profiles, for example through likes. The study showed that varying amounts of attention can also result in ostracism, resulting in negative effects on need-threat and mood, similar to Cyberball.

This is useful for our project as it moves ostracism closer to everyday digital social interaction. As more and more social contact takes place in digital environments, social inclusion is often quantified through small signals such as likes and comments. Even though a digital interface might seem cold and neutral, the signals these interfaces display can still carry a huge emotional weight. It can make a social interaction feel very personal but also very uncertain. It can cause the user to start questioning the situation. Am I being ignored, did the system cause this, or am I imagining it? We wanted to work with this anxious feeling of uncertainty. We wanted to create a situation in which people feel excluded but cannot tell whether this exclusion comes from other people, from the system or from their own interpretation.

# From insight to experience

Our goal was not to explain cyber ostracism but to let visitors feel it, so we built an interactive installation in which the visitor is a participant rather than a spectator. A game was a natural way to place several people in the same controlled social situation, which is also why the existing paradigms, Cyberball and Ostracism Online, are themselves games. It lets us script when and how exclusion happens, repeat it for every group of visitors, and give players a shared goal that makes them want to be included in the first place.  
Framing the experience as a game also introduced a risk we discussed with developmental psychologist Wouter Wolf: a game can create a “magic circle,” a protective sense that “it is only a game”, which gives players emotional distance and weakens the feeling we want to evoke. Three design decisions follow from this. The exclusion must not be attributable to the rules, so it has to happen even when a player is genuinely trying to cooperate; otherwise the experience becomes strategic rather than social and the sting disappears. Competition and explicit justification both weaken the response, so we kept the competition light and gave players no rational explanation for being excluded. Finally, communication between players had to be limited, because being able to talk would let them resolve the uncertainty the experience depends on. Because Cyberball shows that minimal cues are enough to make someone feel ostracised, we did not need a complex simulation, only a small group of real people, a shared goal, and a way to make each privately believe the others were leaving them out. The solution was to give each player their own screen and let the system control what every player sees.

# Our statement

The statement we settled on is: exclusion is impersonal. At first this sounds contradictory, because being excluded feels like one of the most personal experiences there is. That tension is exactly what makes it compelling. The installation is built so that every player feels personally singled out, while in reality no one is being singled out at all: all three are subjected to the same manipulation at the same time. The painful feeling of being the odd one out is produced by the interface, not by any real social decision made about that particular person.  
This leaves room for contemplation rather than delivering a simple message. It invites visitors to reconsider their own moments of feeling ignored online and to ask how many were genuine rejections and how many were gaps in meaning they filled in themselves. The title captures it directly: everyone ends up being the odd bird out, which is only possible if the exclusion was never really about them as individuals.

# The work: Odd Bird Out

Odd Bird Out is a three-player game installation. The players stand around a central triangular structure, each at their own tablet, with panels and over-ear headphones that prevent them from seeing each other’s screens or communicating, so the only information they get about the social situation comes through the interface. Spectators watch from the outside; the players are themselves the exhibit. The work uses a light, ostrich-themed pixel-art style. Players take the role of ostriches collecting eggs. Every round, players are given the option to partner up with one of the other two players. If two players select each other, they each get an egg (essentially \+1 to their score), leaving one player to not get any eggs that round. If each player votes for a different player (in a kind of three-way partnership), all players get a \+1. Mock-ups of the artstyle and screenshots of the prototype can be found in the appendix.

The heart of the installation is a controlled deception on a central server. Because no player can see the others’ screens, the server can show each tablet a different reality across three phases. In the trust phase the game behaves honestly, so players learn it is “real”. In the shift phase the server stops broadcasting the true actions and generates a tailored reality for each player in which the other two appear to cooperate exclusively and reject the one watching: A sees B and C ally against A, B sees A and C against B, and C sees A and B against C. Each player is therefore convinced that they, specifically, are being left out. The experience ends with a reveal, a closing message along the lines of “You were left out of the flock — but so was everyone else,” which exposes the manipulation and turns a private feeling of exclusion into a shared realization.

During the shift phase, a player watches the other two ostriches repeatedly cooperate while their own attempts to share go unanswered, with no way to check what is really happening. This reproduces the gap described earlier: the player cannot tell whether the others are deliberately excluding them, whether the system is limiting their actions, or whether they are reading too much into a few unlucky rounds. The uncertainty, not just the exclusion itself, is what generates the discomfort. Because a player who is honestly cooperating is rejected anyway, the feeling stays social rather than strategic and breaks through the protective “magic circle” of play, following the lesson from Cyberball and from our conversation with Wouter Wolf that very little is needed to make someone feel ostracised as long as the exclusion seems to come from other people. The reveal then reframes everything: the moment a player learns that everyone felt singled out, the personal sting gives way to reflection on how easily an interface can manufacture a rejection that no real person ever intended.

# Reflection

The main tension we faced is that the elements which make the installation fun and approachable, competition and a clear goal, are also the ones that can weaken the feeling of ostracism by giving players a rational explanation for being left out. We addressed this by keeping the competition light, removing any explicit reference to “ostracism” in the game, and designing the manipulation so that cooperation is rejected even when genuinely offered. We cannot claim the exhibition reproduces the measured effects of a controlled study, since the setting is informal and the exposure short, but the experience is grounded in established paradigms that consistently show how little manipulation is needed to make people feel excluded. Odd Bird Out therefore conveys a clear insight, that exclusion can be felt without being explicitly stated, into a short shared experience: by giving three players a private, manipulated view of the same game, it makes everyone the odd bird out at once and then reveals that no one was ever truly singled out. This feeds into the discussion that our statement ultimately intends to provoke in people: exclusion is impersonal.
